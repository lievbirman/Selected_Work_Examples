Boltzmann Factors

The Boltzmann factor gives you the probability of finding a system in a state s characterized by energy E given that the system is in thermal equilibrium with a large reservoir. A surprising number of systems can be studied with this tool. That’s because most systems are not isolated and in fact connected to reservoirs of energy such as gasses, stars, chemical soups, etc. This quantity also connects the macroscopic concepts of thermodynamics directly to the statistics and randomness of physical systems. We’ll use the Boltzmann factor to predict things about biological systems, atoms, and even our atmosphere. We’ll also use this equilibrium idea to study non-equilibrium scenarios. But, before we explore these applications we’ll make the fundamental connection between the statistics and random motion of many tiny things and the macroscopic concepts of thermodynamics.

The physical systems that we care about have two very important intrinsic properties on which the applicability of the Boltzmann factor depends. If a system we’re studying strays away from these conditions then the Boltzmann factor starts to lose its applicability.

The first is that our system is equally likely to be in any microscopically specified state. If we consider a gas, then a single microscopically specified state would be a complete specification of all of the positions and momenta of every single particle at some instant (Don’t worry, we’ll never actually need to make such a detailed specification). This assumption is called the equiprobability assumption. Calculating the probability to be in any unique configuration is done simply by dividing 1 by the total number of configurations in the system.

The second is that the system is very large. How large? Think on the order of degrees of freedom. Actually, we’ll treat seemingly smaller systems but implicitly there will always be some larger system connected to it. The size requirement won’t be very clear now but this has to do with the central limit theorem and how each of the many degrees of freedom work together to get very precise average behavior. We won’t have a full discussion of the central limit theorem here. Rather, we’ll explore the probabilities of a model system that we assume behaves according to the equiprobability assumption.

The system we’ll use was first proposed in 1907 by Einstein and it is a simplistic model of a solid. A solid is an ordered structure that’s connected in a lattice. Essentially, all of the atoms oscillate at discrete energies and we treat them mathematically as oscillators. What we’ll do now is study this model of a solid with some number of atoms and enumerate the number of ways we can arrange energy among these atoms. Then, we’ll take two solids like this and put them into thermal contact. Then we’ll wait. If our model is right we should correctly predict what happens at thermal equilibrium in a real solid.

Large systems have exactly this average behavior when we just let them be and don’t push or “drive” them in any way. This is called equilibrium. A single system is at equilibrium when its behavior is representative of the most probable macrostate with a small margin of error. Two systems, A and B, are at equilibrium with each other when they together reach the macrostate with the greatest value. Macroscopically we can easily observe systems coming into equilibrium with each other. The simplest case of this is taking two objects with different temperatures and bringing them into thermal contact (as we just did). We’ll eventually find that they reach the same temperature. What we just found a moment ago is the equilibrium condition but from the point of view of the microscopic picture. Conceptually we bridged the gap between thermodynamics and statistics.

We have these two notions of equilibrium. The statistical one where we are in the most probable macrostate Ω and the thermodynamic one where we are at the same temperature T as some other system, this is not a coincidence. We’ll use the equivalence between these two ideas to bridge the gap between the two paradigms and get the Boltzmann factor. From there, we’ll jump into solving problems with atomic, biological, and even atmospheric systems. Why is this possible? It’s because many systems satisfy the assumptions we made to get the Boltzmann factor. Namely, that the reservoir and the system contain many degrees of freedom (this is the precise way of saying very large) and that the system as a whole obeys the equiprobability assumption. Q: Non-Equilibrium Thermodynamics

Non-equilibrium thermodynamics is characterized by irreversibility and the loss of useful energy into useless heat. We’ll use a non-rigorous argument to reach a very fundamental result in this field called the Crooks Fluctuation Relation. In a sense, it quantifies why we often see systems propagating in one direction as opposed to the other. The common example used is a cup falling and breaking but never doing the reverse process.
